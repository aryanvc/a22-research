# Google Barges

**Status: Dead (2014; last barge scrapped 2016).** The famous "floating datacenter" that never was — the barges were retail showrooms, and the actual floating-datacenter concept behind the speculation was never built. Still instructive: it's the cleanest case study of maritime regulation killing an ocean project.

## The patent (the real datacenter concept)

Google filed a "Water-Based Data Center" patent in Feb 2007 (granted 2009, US7525207B2): floating datacenters 3–7 miles offshore, powered by wave-energy converters, cooled by seawater heat exchangers, ~40 MW, no real estate or property taxes, movable to demand (disaster relief, military). **Never built by anyone at Google.** The patent is the only reason the world assumed the 2013 mystery barges were datacenters.

## What they actually built

Four oceangoing barges (built 2010–2012 in Louisiana), leased through shell entity "By and Large LLC" ($80K/month for 727,000 sq ft at Treasure Island, SF). Two got 4-story shipping-container superstructures — one in SF Bay, one in Portland, Maine. Per WSJ-FOIA'd documents and Google's lone official statement, they were **invitation-only interactive showrooms** for Google Glass and Google X products — "an interactive space where people can learn about new technology," with plans for a dozen sails and "nautical whimsy."

## How it died

- **US Coast Guard fire safety killed it, quietly, before the public mystery even peaked.** Repeated objections through 2013: 5,000+ gallons of fuel on the main deck, combustible container structure, no way to evacuate a 4-story structure without occupants jumping overboard. Work stopped September 2013. Cancellation was attributed to the cost of meeting federal maritime safety regulations.
- **SF Bay regulators (BCDC) delivered the public blow**: ruled in early 2014 the construction lacked a required permit; Google towed the barge to Stockton (outside BCDC jurisdiction) rather than fight, paying ~$10K/month for it to sit untouched.
- Maine barge sold and scrapped Aug 2014. Stockton barge sold to Boyer Towing in 2016; superstructure demolished in Seattle May 2016.

## Lesson for the segment

Even a non-datacenter, low-risk use of a barge in a US harbor drew two separate regulatory kill shots (Coast Guard fire code, state bay-conservation permitting). Any US coastal/floating compute pitch needs a real answer on maritime permitting — this is the graveyard's clearest evidence that the hard part is not the technology.
